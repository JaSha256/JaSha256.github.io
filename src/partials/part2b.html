Part 2b: The Case For Age Verification — A Fair Assessment ‹

The preceding sections have documented substantial risks of mandatory age verification via EUDI Wallet. Intellectual honesty requires that the strongest arguments for age verification be presented with equal rigor. This section steel-mans the pro-verification position before identifying where it is contested. The aim is not to dismiss these arguments but to engage them seriously and locate the genuine points of disagreement.

2b.1 The Scale of Online Child Exploitation

The harms that age verification proponents seek to address are not hypothetical. The Internet Watch Foundation (IWF) confirmed 275,652 URLs containing child sexual abuse imagery in 2023 alone, with 93% being self-generated material — children coerced into producing images through direct social media contact.42 NCMEC CyberTipline reports rose from approximately 100,000 in 2010 to 36.2 million in 2023 (20.5 million in 2024 following a reporting methodology change); in 2024 the CyberTipline received nearly 63 million individual files. Financial sextortion reports to the CyberTipline tripled between 2021 and 2023, with one in six reports mentioning self-harm or suicide.

These harms occur through the basic functionality of user accounts and direct messaging — not through algorithmic amplification. A determined adult can contact a child if both have accounts on the same platform, regardless of how the recommendation algorithm is configured. This is the strongest argument against relying exclusively on design regulation: design changes cannot prevent grooming if both parties have access to the same platform.

Sources: IWF Annual Report 2023; Thorn, “Child Sexual Exploitation and Technology.”

The Displacement Problem: Encrypted Messaging

A further structural limitation of age verification deserves acknowledgment by its proponents: the displacement problem posed by end-to-end encrypted (E2EE) messaging. WhatsApp (~2 billion users globally), Signal, and iMessage encrypt communications such that the platform operator cannot inspect content. Age verification operates at the platform’s “front door” — controlling who may create an account. But once an adult and a child are connected on a platform, grooming can migrate to encrypted direct messages where no content-based intervention is possible, or to entirely separate E2EE services. This is not a hypothetical: the National Crime Agency (UK) and Europol have documented migration of CSAM distribution from open platforms to encrypted channels (Telegram, Matrix, peer-to-peer) following platform-level enforcement. Age-verifying the front door of Instagram does not affect communications on WhatsApp — both owned by Meta, yet subject to fundamentally different technical constraints.65

The displacement problem connects directly to the EU CSAM Regulation debate (Section 2.4): the proposed “Chat Control” regulation attempts to address this gap by mandating client-side scanning of encrypted messages — illustrating the policy-ratchet logic. Age verification that cannot reach encrypted communications creates pressure for the next intervention that can, leading to proposals that undermine encryption itself.

2b.2 The Mental Health Debate: Neither Settled Nor Dismissible

The scientific evidence on social media’s impact on youth mental health is genuinely contested. This analysis must be transparent about the state of the debate rather than selectively citing one side.

The alarm position (Haidt, 2024; Twenge, 2017): A synchronized international rise in adolescent anxiety, depression, and self-harm beginning around 2012–2013 coincides with smartphone and social media saturation. Haidt reports correlations of r = .15–.20 for girls specifically (figures from his popular book; peer-reviewed meta-analyses such as Hancock et al., 2022, report smaller effect sizes), and cites quasi-experimental evidence that high-speed internet rollout produced 90% increases in mental health diagnoses for girls in Spain. The US Surgeon General issued a formal advisory in 2023 stating there is “not enough evidence to determine that social media is sufficiently safe for children.”43

The skeptical position (Orben & Przybylski, 2019): Specification curve analysis across 355,358 adolescents found digital technology use explained at most 0.4% of variance in well-being — comparable to the effect of wearing glasses or eating potatoes. Their core methodological point: analytical flexibility allows researchers to produce dramatically different results from the same data.44

The middle ground (Valkenburg et al., 2022; Beyens et al., 2020): The differential susceptibility model shows that the average effect is near zero, but this masks enormous individual variation. Some adolescents are meaningfully harmed, some benefit, and most show no measurable effect. The field lacks consensus, and interpretation depends on one’s threshold for what constitutes a meaningful effect.45

The best available meta-analytic estimate: Hancock, Liu & French (2022) conducted a comprehensive meta-analysis of 226 studies (n > 275,000 participants). The overall association between social media use and a combined measure of well-being was r = 0.01 (not statistically significant), but this aggregate masks meaningful variation across subdimensions: small positive associations with depression (r = .12, p < .01) and anxiety (r = .13, p < .01), and a stronger positive association with social well-being (r = .20, p < .01). The most accurate characterization: the relationship is small, dimension-dependent, and near zero in aggregate. This is the most methodologically rigorous aggregation of the evidence to date and should be positioned as the baseline against which both alarm and skeptical claims are evaluated.73

The honest assessment: Neither side is clearly right. Social media likely has a real but modest average effect that is substantially larger for specific subgroups (girls, pre-existing vulnerability, heavy users, younger adolescents). The mechanisms are more complex than simple dose-response. The APA’s 2023 advisory took a nuanced position: social media is “not inherently beneficial or harmful” but “poses risks” that depend on individual characteristics.

Post-2022 developments: Since the debate’s initial polarization, the evidence base has continued to evolve. Orben et al. (2024) replicated and extended the specification curve approach with a global dataset (n>400,000 adolescents across 35 countries), finding that the association between social media use and life satisfaction remains small and inconsistent across countries — undermining claims of a universal causal mechanism. Coyne et al. (2024, Child Development) found in a longitudinal panel study that social media use at baseline did not predict depression or anxiety two years later, after controlling for baseline mental health — the strongest evidence against a simple causal link. However, the US Surgeon General (2024) issued a call for warning labels on social media platforms, arguing that even small population-level effects translate into large absolute numbers given the scale of use. The field remains divided, but the methodological quality of skeptical research has strengthened since 2022.66

2b.3 Digital Identity Systems That Have Not Been Abused

The analysis in Part 2 draws heavily on negative precedents (South Korea, China, Turkey, Russia). Fairness requires acknowledging that not all digital identity systems have been misused:

Estonia’s e-ID has operated for over 20 years with ~99% population coverage and no documented case of systematic surveillance abuse. Citizens can see who accessed their data and when — a transparency mechanism that has led to prosecution of unauthorized access. The 2017 ROCA vulnerability (~800,000 affected cards) was discovered through proactive security research and resolved through certificate suspension and renewal, demonstrating the system’s self-correcting capacity.46

Norway’s BankID reaches ~95% of the adult population (~85% of total population including minors) and has been used for age verification (including at Vinmonopolet, the state alcohol monopoly) since 2024. No systematic privacy abuse has been documented in 20 years of operation. However, BankID is an online verification model where the identity provider (the bank) is involved in every authentication transaction, creating a centralized correlation point — structurally similar to the wallet provider tracking concern raised in Section 1.3. Academic research has identified protocol-level vulnerabilities and the system reveals full identity rather than enabling selective disclosure.47

India’s Aadhaar presents the most complex picture. On the positive side: 1.4 billion enrollments, bank account ownership rising from 35% to 80% of adults, and an estimated $33 billion saved through elimination of ghost beneficiaries (World Bank). On the negative side: 49% of beneficiaries in Jharkhand experiencing at least one biometric authentication failure during the study period (Dreze et al., 2017) — not a per-transaction failure rate, but still a damning figure for welfare delivery that excluded the most vulnerable populations, and multiple data breaches including one where database access was purchasable for ~$6.48

Further positive cases: Denmark’s NemID/MitID and Sweden’s BankID (~8 million active users) have been used for age-gated services for years without documented systematic surveillance abuse. These Nordic cases represent the most relevant comparison class for the EUDI proposal — democratic states with strong rule of law and mature digital identity infrastructure — and their largely positive track record must be weighed against the negative precedents cited in Part 2.

The key distinction: Estonia’s success rests on a distributed data architecture (X-Road connecting sector-specific registries, avoiding a single monolithic identity store), citizen-visible access logs, open-source infrastructure, and 28 years of incremental trust-building. The EUDI Wallet faces a hard 2026 deadline across 27 member states with heterogeneous institutional cultures — including states with documented democratic backsliding. The question is not whether digital identity can work (Estonia proves it can) but whether the specific architecture and political context of the EUDI Wallet provide adequate safeguards.

2b.4 Privacy-Preserving Age Verification: A Technically Feasible Alternative?

The analysis in Part 1 demonstrates that the current EUDI Wallet architecture fails to provide anonymity. But proponents argue that privacy-preserving age verification is technically feasible:

The CNIL “double anonymity” model (France): A three-party architecture where the age issuer knows the user’s identity but not which site they visit; an intermediary knows the site but not the user’s identity; and the platform knows only that the age requirement is met. Cryptographic blinding and organizational separation ensure no party holds both identity and usage data. The privacy guarantee comes from architectural role separation combined with blinded tokens, not from zero-knowledge proofs in the formal cryptographic sense.49

On-device age estimation: Neural networks running entirely on the user’s device estimate age from a selfie; the result (over/under threshold) is attested and sent to the platform. No image or biometric data leaves the device. However, this approach faces an “attestation trilemma”: it requires (a) on-device processing for privacy, (b) remote attestation for integrity (preventing the user from simply claiming to be over 18), and (c) no trusted third party for decentralization — at most two of these can be achieved simultaneously. In practice, attestation integrity depends on hardware trust anchors (Apple/Google attestation services), reintroducing a platform dependency. Recent systems (e.g., Yoti) report mean absolute error of ~1.5 years for ages 13–17, suggesting improving accuracy, though the trust anchor problem remains.

The critical question this analysis must address: If a technically well-implemented, privacy-preserving age verification system existed — one using ZKP-based selective disclosure with no linkability, no data retention, and no centralized de-anonymization — would the democratic and fundamental rights objections in Part 2 still hold?

The answer is: partially. Even a technically perfect system creates an infrastructure of verification that did not previously exist. The function creep risk (Section 2.4) applies to the infrastructure, not the specific implementation. A system built for age verification today can be extended to verify other attributes tomorrow — political affiliation, health status, immigration status — through the same institutional channels. Moreover, on-device age estimation still requires the norm that accessing public digital spaces requires proving something about oneself — a norm that, once established, is difficult to constrain. But the degree of concern is substantially lower for a well-designed ZKP system than for the current EUDI Wallet architecture. The analysis’s critique is strongest against the specific proposal (EUDI Wallet with signed credentials), not against the abstract concept of privacy-preserving age attestation.

2b.5 The Bootstrapping Problem of Design Regulation

Proponents of age verification raise a structural objection to this paper’s preferred alternative (design regulation): design regulation cannot protect children it cannot identify. The UK Children’s Code sets privacy defaults to “high” for users the platform knows are children. But a child who lies about their age at signup — claiming to be 18 — receives no AADC protections whatsoever. The Code does not require age verification; it assumes platforms already know which users are children.50

This is a genuine weakness in the design-regulation-only approach. The rebuttal must be honest: design regulation works best for systemic design features (disabling algorithmic amplification for all users, removing infinite scroll by default, requiring opt-in to personalized feeds) that protect everyone regardless of age. It works less well for age-differentiated protections (restricting DMs, hiding certain content categories) that require knowing the user’s age.

Existing Legal Obligations and Parental Rights

Two further pro-verification arguments deserve acknowledgment. First, the Audiovisual Media Services Directive (AVMSD, 2018) already requires age verification for on-demand video services showing pornographic content. Platforms face legal liability for knowingly exposing minors to harmful content; without some form of age assurance, they cannot know who is a minor. This is not a hypothetical concern but an existing legal obligation. Second, the UN CRC General Comment No. 25 (2021, paras. 19–22) recognizes parents’ right and duty to guide children’s digital environment. Age verification can be seen as a tool enabling parental oversight — a consideration this paper’s emphasis on children’s agency (Interim section) should not obscure.

The optimal regulatory mix may therefore include some form of age assurance — but the proportionality principle demands that it be the least invasive form possible, and the current EUDI Wallet proposal does not meet that standard.51

The UK Online Safety Act as a “do both” test case: The UK’s Online Safety Act 2023 represents the most ambitious attempt to combine design regulation (the AADC) with age verification requirements (Part 5, §§11–12). Ofcom’s draft Codes of Practice (published March 2025) require age assurance for services likely to be accessed by children, alongside continued enforcement of design standards. Early implementation reveals the tensions this paper predicts: platforms have focused compliance resources on age-gating mechanisms rather than on the more costly systemic design changes the AADC requires — precisely the moral licensing dynamic described in the Interim section. The UK experience will provide the first large-scale empirical test of whether combining both approaches produces additive protection or substitution effects.67
