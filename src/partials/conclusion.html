Conclusion ‹

This analysis set out to examine whether mandatory age verification via the EUDI Wallet is a proportionate response to the documented harms minors face on social media. Its core finding is that the architecture specified in ARF v2.8 — using SD-JWT/mdoc credentials with static issuer signatures, with batch issuance as the primary unlinkability mitigation and BBS/ZKP-based selective disclosure still absent from mandatory requirements — fails to deliver the privacy-preserving age verification that proponents promise. Pseudonyms are generated locally rather than through a centralized intermediary, which reduces one category of structural risk, but the fundamental unlinkability deficit persists: batch issuance mitigates correlability without eliminating it. The technology does not match the policy rhetoric.

Applying the four-step proportionality test under Art. 52(1) EU Charter, universal EUDI-based age verification fails on necessity and proportionality stricto sensu. Less invasive alternatives exist and are demonstrably effective: the UK AADC achieved 91 significant design changes without requiring any identification infrastructure; the EDPB recommends age assurance over age verification; privacy-preserving approaches (CNIL model, on-device estimation, ZKP-based attestation) can provide age confidence without linking government identity to content access. The marginal child protection benefit of universal age verification over these alternatives does not justify the creation of permanent mass identity infrastructure with documented chilling effects, function creep risks, and structural tensions with the eIDAS Regulation’s own voluntariness principle.

This paper is pro-better-technology, not anti-technology. It advocates for privacy-preserving age assurance mechanisms that meet the EDPB’s verification/assurance distinction — combined with robust design regulation that addresses the root causes of harm. The surveillance advertising business model, not the identity of users, drives algorithmic amplification, addictive design, and the downstream harms to democratic integrity, public health, and epistemic quality documented in Part 3. Regulating who accesses a platform without simultaneously addressing what the platform does misallocates regulatory priority.

The regulatory priority should be to close the DSA enforcement deficit, advance the Digital Fairness Act, and direct enforcement resources at platform design rather than user identity. Concretely: binding timelines for researcher access under DSA Art. 40, dedicated enforcement budgets commensurate with the scale of regulated platforms, redistribution of enforcement authority away from single national bottlenecks, and sanctions for platform non-compliance with data access requests. Adding new instruments subject to the same enforcement constraints — the same resource asymmetry, technical opacity, and jurisdictional bottlenecks documented in Section 3.3 — does not solve the institutional capacity problem. It compounds it.

Acknowledged Limitations

This analysis does not resolve every tension it identifies. Design regulation cannot prevent grooming through basic platform functionality (Section 2b.1) — a genuine gap that some form of age assurance may need to address. The enforcement deficit affects all regulatory instruments, including this paper’s preferred alternative. The proportionality analysis is conducted as a policy assessment, not a binding legal opinion — it lacks the specific safeguard analysis a court would require. And the paper’s conclusion aligns with the position of the surveillance advertising industry itself, which opposes age verification mandates — an alignment that should prompt reflection on whether the argument inadvertently serves the interests of the platforms it criticizes. The argument is not that no form of age assurance is ever justified, but that the specific proposal — mandatory EUDI Wallet-based identification for all social media users — fails the proportionality test against less invasive alternatives that address root causes.
