Interim Consideration: Proportionality Analysis Under Art. 52(1) EU Charter ‹

Having presented both the risks (Part 2) and the strongest arguments for age verification (Part 2b), this section conducts a structured proportionality analysis under Art. 52(1) of the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights. Art. 52(1) requires that any limitation on fundamental rights must (1) pursue a legitimate aim recognized by the Union, (2) be suitable for achieving that aim, (3) be necessary — i.e., no less restrictive alternative achieves the same objective — and (4) be proportionate stricto sensu — i.e., the benefits must not be manifestly disproportionate to the costs. This four-step test structures the analysis that follows.

Step 1: Legitimate Aim — Conceded

The protection of minors from online harms is a legitimate aim recognized by the Union. Art. 24(2) of the EU Charter requires that “in all actions relating to children, whether taken by public authorities or private institutions, the child’s best interests must be a primary consideration.” This is a Treaty-level obligation. The documented harms are real: according to Meta’s own internal research (disclosed by Haugen, 2021; not independently peer-reviewed), among British teens who already experienced suicidal thoughts, 13% traced them to Instagram; 17% of affected teens reported worsened eating disorders; and 32% of teen girls who felt bad about their bodies reported feeling worse when using Instagram. The IWF confirmed 275,652 URLs containing child sexual abuse imagery in 2023 alone (Section 2b.1). This analysis does not contest the legitimacy of the aim.

Step 2: Suitability — Conceded with Reservations

The suitability test asks whether mandatory EUDI-based age verification is capable of achieving the stated objective. Under established CJEU doctrine, this is a low threshold: a measure is suitable if it is capable of contributing to the objective at all. The evidence suggests marginal but non-zero effectiveness:

  • South Korea (Section 2.1): The most comprehensive natural experiment yielded a decline in harmful comments of 0.9 percentage points (from 13.9% to 13.0%) — alongside 35 million compromised data records.
  • Australia (Online Safety Amendment Act 2024): VPN-based circumvention was documented within weeks of the announcement. The eSafety Commissioner’s technical trial found that no system achieved both high accuracy (>95%) and low friction simultaneously.68
  • France (Loi SREN, 2024): ARCOM has struggled to find a technically compliant solution meeting the Conseil constitutionnel’s strict privacy conditions — validating that the gap between privacy-preserving theory and deployment reality is substantial.69
  • UK (Online Safety Act 2023): Ofcom acknowledged that “no single age assurance technology” meets all requirements and adopted a technology-neutral approach, effectively conceding the implementation trilemma.70

The fundamental suitability problem is that age verification addresses access, not harm. The documented harms — algorithmic amplification, addictive design, engagement-maximizing recommendation systems — operate on users after they access the platform. An age-verified 16-year-old on the SPD’s “youth version” remains exposed to the same business model dynamics unless the platform’s design is separately regulated.

The suitability test is met. Age verification does restrict some minors’ access — 0.9% is marginal, but not zero, and the juridical threshold requires only that the measure can contribute to the objective. Conceding suitability, however, sharpens the analysis at the subsequent steps: the low marginal effectiveness becomes directly relevant to the necessity assessment (are less invasive alternatives equally or more effective?) and to proportionality stricto sensu (do the costs justify a marginal benefit?).

Step 3: Necessity — Fails

The necessity test is the most consequential: are there less invasive alternatives that achieve the same legitimate objective?

Design regulation as a less invasive alternative: The UK Age Appropriate Design Code (AADC) achieved 91 significant design changes on major platforms without requiring any identification infrastructure (Section 3.3). Instagram set all under-16 accounts to private; TikTok stopped nighttime push notifications for under-18s; YouTube disabled autoplay for children. These changes protect children systemically — regardless of whether the platform knows the precise age of each user — and they address the mechanisms of harm (algorithmic amplification, addictive features) rather than merely restricting access.

Privacy-preserving age assurance: The EDPB’s Statement 1/2025 on Age Assurance explicitly distinguishes between age verification (precise age determination via identity documents) and age assurance (reasonable confidence about age group). The EDPB recommends the latter where possible, noting that verification should be reserved for services where access is legally restricted to adults (gambling, pornography). Less invasive age assurance mechanisms include:36

  • The CNIL “double anonymity” model (Section 2b.4): architectural role separation ensuring no party holds both identity and usage data
  • On-device age estimation: neural networks running entirely on the user’s device, with no biometric data leaving the device
  • Privacy-preserving attribute attestation using zero-knowledge proofs (BBS signatures, as recommended by the cryptographers’ joint statement)

DSA enforcement: The Digital Services Act already prohibits profile-based advertising for known minors (Art. 28(2)), mandates algorithmic transparency (Art. 27), and bans dark patterns (Art. 25). Art. 28(3) explicitly states that platforms should not be incentivized to collect additional personal data to determine whether a user is a minor. The appropriate response to the DSA enforcement deficit is to close it — through dedicated funding, binding researcher access timelines, and redistribution of enforcement authority — not to introduce additional instruments subject to the same constraints.

Existing German law: Germany already requires age verification for specific categories of demonstrably harmful content (pornography, extreme violence) under the JMStV, §§ 4–5. The SPD proposal’s extension to all social media use for all content represents a qualitative leap from targeted content restriction to general identification — fundamentally altering the proportionality calculus.40

The necessity test fails because less invasive alternatives exist and are demonstrably effective. Universal EUDI-based age verification is not the least restrictive means of achieving the legitimate objective.

Step 4: Proportionality Stricto Sensu — Fails

Even if necessity were satisfied, the balance of costs and benefits fails the proportionality stricto sensu test:

Documented costs:

  • Mass identity infrastructure linking government credentials to content access for all users, including adults (Part 1)
  • Digital exclusion of populations without digital identity or technical capability (Section 2.5)
  • Contradiction with eIDAS 2.0’s own voluntariness principle, Art. 5a(1) (Section 2.6)

Projected risks (empirically grounded but not directly tested for age verification):

  • Chilling effects on expression, disproportionately affecting minority viewpoints (Section 2.3) — empirically predicted from surveillance contexts but untested for age verification specifically; the hypothesis is formulated in Section 2.3 and awaits direct empirical testing
  • Function creep risk inherent in permanent identity infrastructure without sunset clause (Section 2.4) — structurally favored by institutional incentives but not inevitable, as the COVID Certificate sunset (June 2023) demonstrates

Benefits: Marginal reduction in minors’ access to social media platforms, partially offset by circumvention (VPN, false credentials) and displacement to unregulated services. The benefits do not address the root causes of harm (algorithmic amplification, addictive design, surveillance advertising business model) — these persist regardless of age verification.

The disproportion is clear: a permanent mass identity infrastructure affecting all users in order to achieve a marginal child protection benefit that could be more effectively achieved through design regulation.

Regulatory Resources Are Finite

A further structural argument reinforces the proportionality finding. Regulatory bandwidth, enforcement capacity, and political capital are finite resources. Implementing and enforcing universal age verification consumes resources — technical standards development, platform compliance audits, DPA oversight, judicial review — that could be directed at design regulation and DSA enforcement, which address root causes rather than symptoms.

A secondary concern is the moral licensing effect: once platforms implement an age gate, they can argue that they have fulfilled their duty of care, weakening political pressure for structural reforms. This effect is documented in individual social psychology (Monin & Miller, 2001; Mazar & Zhong, 2010), and the cookie consent analogy is instructive: Nouwens et al. (2020) found that only 11.8% of cookie consent implementations meet GDPR minimum requirements — formal compliance displacing substantive regulation. A fair objection: this argument cuts both ways; design regulation could equally create licensing effects against age verification. Moreover, the transfer from individual psychology to institutional regulatory dynamics is asserted but not empirically established.38

Youth Voice and Agency

Research consistently shows that young people are not passive victims but active agents who develop strategies for navigating online risks (Livingstone & Third, 2017). The UN Committee on the Rights of the Child, General Comment No. 25 (2021), emphasizes that age verification measures “should not compromise children’s rights” to privacy (para. 69) and that “digital inclusion is essential for realizing the full range of children’s rights” (para. 12). Excluding under-14s from social media entirely — as the SPD proposes — restricts not only access but participation, expression, and social connection.39

Could One Not Do Both?

The “do both” response — implement design regulation and age verification — is theoretically appealing and cannot be dismissed categorically. The UK Online Safety Act 2023 represents the most ambitious attempt to combine both approaches; early implementation suggests platforms may focus compliance resources on age-gating rather than the more costly systemic design changes the AADC requires — but this observation is preliminary, as the OSA’s implementation is still in its early stages and definitive conclusions about substitution effects would be premature. The moral licensing concern — that formal compliance with age verification reduces political pressure for structural reforms — is plausible but rests on individual-level psychological findings (Monin & Miller, 2001) whose transfer to institutional regulatory dynamics is asserted, not empirically established.

“Do both” remains theoretically possible, and its viability depends on implementation details. The critical constraint is this: if both approaches are pursued simultaneously, age verification must take its least invasive form — privacy-preserving age assurance rather than identity-linked verification. The EUDI Wallet as currently specified (ARF v1.4/1.5) does not meet this requirement. A “do both” strategy that pairs design regulation with EUDI-based identification is not complementary but compounding — it adds the costs of identity infrastructure to those of design enforcement without evidence that the combination produces benefits exceeding either approach alone.
