Part 1: Technical Analysis of the EUDI Wallet Architecture ‹

1.1 EUDI Wallet — Pseudonymous, Not Anonymous

The SPD calls for age verification via EUDI Wallet and promises “no restriction of anonymity.” The technical reality fundamentally contradicts this promise.

The Problem: Signed Credentials

Germany relies on the so-called signed credentials model: identity data is cryptographically signed — i.e., digitally certified — by a government authority and then transmitted to the respective recipient. Since the signature remains valid beyond the original transaction context (as long as the credential is unrevoked and the cryptographic primitives hold), any recipient can pass the signed data on to third parties unchanged — it remains verifiable without the holder being able to detect or prevent this. Cryptographers and data protection authorities have explicitly warned against this model.

“An issue with any digital credential is that it can easily be copied and thus illegally distributed.”

— Slamanig, D. (2025). “Privacy-Preserving Authentication: Theory vs. Practice.”1

Unlinkability (Nichtverkettbarkeit) Is Not Guaranteed in the Current Design

Both data formats specified in the current Architecture Reference Framework (ARF v2.8, February 2025) — SD-JWT and ISO 18013-5/mdoc — use static, issuer-generated digital signatures over the (hashed) identity attributes of the user. This signature functions as a persistent identifier — it remains identical with each presentation. Any two service providers (so-called Relying Parties) can therefore link transactions of the same user with minimal effort. SD-JWT provides selective disclosure through a hash-based mechanism that allows the holder to reveal only chosen attributes, but the underlying issuer signature remains constant across presentations — selective disclosure is not the same as unlinkability.

“the signature as well as (h1,...,hn) are always revealed and thus can be trivially linked by any RP.”

— Slamanig, D. (2025).1 Keynote, 19th IFIP Summer School on Privacy and Identity Management.

Joint Statement by International Cryptographers

In June 2024, 16 internationally renowned cryptographers published a joint statement documenting the fundamental deficiencies of the ARF (then at v1.4), filed as GitHub Issue #200.2 The issue remains open and unresolved — none of the recommended changes have been incorporated into subsequent ARF versions, including v2.8. Signatories include, among others:

  • Anna Lysyanskaya (Brown University) — co-inventor of CL signatures (Camenisch-Lysyanskaya, CRYPTO 2001/2004)
  • Jan Camenisch (Dfinity) — co-inventor of CL signatures, IBM Idemix
  • Bart Preneel (KU Leuven) — one of the most cited cryptographers worldwide
  • Carmela Troncoso (EPFL) — led the DP-3T protocol for COVID contact tracing
  • Anja Lehmann (Hasso Plattner Institute, University of Potsdam)
  • Daniel Slamanig (Universität der Bundeswehr München)

Their core criticism: The technology to ensure anonymity has existed for over 20 years (BBS signatures, CL signatures, U-Prove). The ARF architects deliberately chose against it. BBS (as being standardized by IRTF CFRG) enables unlinkable proof of knowledge of a valid signature — the holder derives a cryptographically fresh, unlinkable zero-knowledge proof per presentation rather than revealing the issuer’s static signature — combined with zero-knowledge selective disclosure. The current ARF architecture cannot do this. Neither BBS signatures nor zero-knowledge proofs appear anywhere in ARF v2.8 — not in the normative text, not in the credential format specifications, not even as a future consideration. Notably, U-Prove (Microsoft Research) operates on standard elliptic curves (including P-256) without requiring pairings, demonstrating that anonymous credentials are not inherently incompatible with existing hardware — though U-Prove offers more limited selective disclosure than BBS and its development has been largely discontinued.59

Batch Issuance: Mandatory but Insufficient

The ARF acknowledges the linkability problem and mandates batch issuance as its primary mitigation: the issuer provides the holder with multiple copies of the same credential, each bearing a distinct signature. The holder uses a different copy per presentation, preventing cross-verifier correlation via signature comparison. ARF v2.8 makes batch issuance mandatory and adds specific safeguards: requirement ISSU_35 mandates that unique elements have negligible collision probability; VCR_17 requires random index assignment in status lists to prevent correlation; and VCR_18 requires “herd privacy” through sufficiently large status lists. Despite these measures, the ARF itself acknowledges that batch issuance can only “partly mitigate” relying party linkability.30

Cryptographers consider batch issuance an inadequate substitute for proper unlinkable proof schemes. The number of unlinkable presentations is bounded by batch size — once all copies are consumed, the holder must contact the issuer for re-issuance, creating new tracking vectors (ISSU_45 triggers provider contact when the unused credential count drops below a threshold). Despite the VCR_17/VCR_18 mitigations, the status list provider can still observe access patterns (timing, IP addresses) when revocation status is checked — a statistical rather than cryptographic privacy guarantee. Privacy-preserving revocation mechanisms (e.g., accumulator-based revocation as in CL credentials) would provide stronger guarantees but were not adopted. Several EU-funded research projects (OLYMPUS, IMPULSE) have developed and tested privacy-preserving credential systems — yet their results were not integrated into the ARF.

Hardware Constraints: BBS signatures require pairing-friendly elliptic curves (primarily BLS12-381, as specified in the IETF draft). Mass-market secure elements in smartphones lack support for pairing-based cryptography — while they support curves beyond P-256 (including P-384, P-521, and in some cases Ed25519), none support the pairing operations BBS requires. The bottleneck is the pairing computation itself, not merely curve support. However, hybrid approaches that delegate pairing operations to software while using the secure element for key storage are being actively developed (cf. IACR ePrint 2025/619, “Making BBS Anonymous Credentials eIDAS 2.0 Compliant”), demonstrating that the hardware constraint is an engineering challenge, not a fundamental barrier.3

Wallet Instance Attestation as a Further Linking Vector: The ARF requires wallet instances to be attested — proving they are genuine, unmodified wallet apps running on secure hardware. ARF v2.8 adds mitigations: requirement OIA_16 mandates that Relying Parties discard unique elements after use, and CIR 2024/2979 Recital (9) recommends “privacy preserving techniques also for the wallet unit attestation,” including “multiple wallet unit attestations for different purposes” and “limit[ing] the lifetime of the wallet unit attestation.” However, these are recommendations (“should,” “may include”), not binding requirements. The structural concern remains: wallet attestations contain public keys and device information that can serve as correlators, and wallet providers and OS platform vendors (Google, Apple) retain a mechanism to correlate transactions across verifiers through the attestation infrastructure.

1.2 Zero-Knowledge Proofs Are Not Mandatory

Zero-knowledge proofs (ZKP) are mentioned in the eIDAS 2.0 Regulation (Regulation (EU) 2024/1183) only in the recitals — not as a binding requirement in the implementing acts. The European Commission has systematically ignored the relevant data protection provisions in the technical implementing specifications. ARF v2.8 does not reference zero-knowledge proofs in its normative requirements, nor do the credential formats (SD-JWT, ISO 18013-5/mdoc) support zero-knowledge selective disclosure.

“The ARF simply ignores this paragraph completely.”

— epicenter.works (2024). Data Protection Analysis: eIDAS Architecture Reference Framework 1.4.4

The epicenter.works analysis was directed at ARF v1.4, but the structural criticism remains applicable: subsequent ARF versions through v2.8 have not incorporated zero-knowledge proof mechanisms. The selective disclosure available via SD-JWT operates through hash-based attribute hiding — the holder can choose which attributes to reveal, but the issuer’s static signature is always disclosed, providing no unlinkability guarantee.

1.3 Lack of Protection Against Tracking by Wallet Providers

ARF v2.8 does not mandate an architecture that prevents wallet providers — or the underlying OS platform (Android/iOS) — from observing transaction metadata. Section 4.3.2 states that the Wallet Provider backend “provides support to the User and collect[s] aggregated and User-consented information in a privacy-preserving manner,” but does not clarify whether presentation logs — which Relying Parties the user interacts with — are visible to the Wallet Provider. Several implementation paths would expose transactions: cloud-based key storage, wallet instance attestation requiring backend communication, and OS-level telemetry that can observe wallet activity independently of the wallet app itself.

“Every use of the wallet could be monitored by the state.”

— Thomas Lohninger, epicenter.works.5

1.4 Local Pseudonyms — An Improvement with Remaining Gaps

ARF v2.8 specifies that pseudonyms are generated and stored locally on the device (Section 2.5), with the Wallet Unit generating pseudonyms that are “specific and unique to that wallet-relying party” (CIR 2024/2979), in alignment with Art. 5a(4)(e) eIDAS. This represents a significant improvement over earlier ARF versions (v1.4/v1.5), which had introduced a centralized “Pseudonym Provider” — an entity that would verify the user’s real identity, issue pseudonyms, maintain a mapping table, and disclose the true identity upon law enforcement request. That design was inconsistent with the Regulation’s intent: Art. 5a(4)(e) and Recital (11c) envision local pseudonym generation on the end device, not a centralized de-anonymization service. Its removal demonstrates that critical scrutiny of identity architectures can drive meaningful improvement.71

However, the shift to local pseudonym generation does not resolve the underlying unlinkability deficit. Pseudonyms provide stable identifiers within a given user-relying-party relationship, but they do not address the cross-verifier linkability created by static issuer signatures in SD-JWT and mdoc credentials. A user presenting age verification to two different platforms still reveals the same cryptographic signature — the pseudonym is a separate layer that does not mask the credential’s structural identifiers. Without unlinkable proof schemes (BBS, CL signatures), local pseudonyms offer relationship pseudonymity but not transaction unlinkability.

“The eID Wallet risks becoming a tool of surveillance and control rather than a tool that empowers users.”

— EDRi, “The eID Wallet still doesn’t deserve your full trust”, 10.03.2026.6

1.5 Article 45 — Mandatory Trust in State Certification Authorities

Article 45 of the eIDAS 2.0 Regulation requires browser manufacturers (Mozilla, Google, Apple) to recognize Qualified Website Authentication Certificates (QWACs) issued by EU member states. The final text (Regulation (EU) 2024/1183) includes a compromise: browsers may continue to apply their own security policies (including CA/Browser Forum Baseline Requirements) in addition to recognizing QWACs, provided they do not discriminate against QWACs compared to other certificates meeting the same standards. Whether this compromise is sufficient remains contested. Over 500 cybersecurity researchers warned in an open letter (November 2023):7

“The current proposal radically expands the ability of governments to surveil both their own citizens and residents across the EU by providing them with the technical means to intercept encrypted web traffic.”

— eidas-open-letter.org (over 500 signatories, including EFF, EDRi, Internet Society, Mozilla, Linux Foundation)

Partial Mitigation Through Certificate Transparency

The implementing regulation (Commission Implementing Regulation (EU) 2025/2527, December 2025, applicable from January 2027) governs QWACs under Article 45(2) eIDAS, referencing ETSI standards that incorporate Certificate Transparency (CT) requirements. QWACs must be logged in publicly accessible CT logs. This significantly raises the bar for covert, large-scale TLS interception — a rogue certificate for gmail.com would appear in public logs and could be detected within hours. However, targeted, short-duration interception (e.g., of a specific journalist’s traffic) remains possible before CT monitors flag the anomaly. The structural concern — that Article 45 undermines browser vendors’ ability to enforce their own security standards — remains valid regardless of CT mitigations.31

Reference: Wazan, A.S. et al. (2024). “Article 45 of the eIDAS Directive Unveils the need to implement the X.509 4-cornered trust model for the WebPKI.” ARES ’24.8

1.6 Google’s Role in the German Prototype Development

In the SPRIND “Funke” competition, Google was admitted to the German wallet prototype development — despite documented data protection concerns within the jury. Google developed the “Android German EUDI Open Source Wallet Prototype” (GitHub: google/funke-eudi-wallet-prototype, since archived).

The involvement raises specific, documented concerns: (1) Google’s production identity stack (Android Credential Manager, Google Identity Services) remains proprietary and closed-source, creating a dependency on a platform whose business model is surveillance advertising; (2) the prototype uses Android’s StrongBox secure element for key storage, which is controlled by Google’s hardware security module — an opaque trust anchor for a European sovereignty infrastructure; (3) Google is simultaneously a Relying Party (as a platform that would verify age) and an infrastructure provider (as the wallet developer), creating a structural conflict of interest that the ARF does not address.52
