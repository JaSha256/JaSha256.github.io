Abstract

In February 2026, senior SPD politicians published a policy paper calling for a social media ban for children under 14, a “youth version” for 14–15-year-olds, and comprehensive age verification via EUDI Wallet — including for adults. Using this proposal as a policy trigger, this analysis examines EUDI-based age verification broadly. It demonstrates that the EUDI Wallet architecture as specified in ARF v2.8 — relying on SD-JWT/mdoc with static issuer signatures and offering batch issuance as a partial unlinkability mitigation rather than mandatory cryptographic unlinkability through BBS signatures or zero-knowledge proofs — fails to deliver the privacy-preserving age verification that proponents promise. A structured proportionality analysis under Art. 52(1) EU Charter of Fundamental Rights finds that universal EUDI-based verification fails the necessity and proportionality stricto sensu tests: less invasive alternatives — privacy-preserving age assurance, design regulation (UK AADC), DSA enforcement — achieve the legitimate child protection objective without creating permanent mass identity infrastructure. The paper advocates a pro-better-technology position: privacy-preserving age assurance combined with robust design regulation that targets the surveillance advertising business model driving online harms, rather than the identity of users.

Keywords: age verification, EUDI Wallet, platform governance, proportionality analysis, surveillance, chilling effects, design regulation, digital identity, contextual integrity, fundamental rights
